# When to Replace Your Cassette (and Chainrings)

Replace your cassette every two to three chains. That's the rule that saves you from the most expensive drivetrain repair: the skip-on-new-chain scenario where a fresh chain immediately hops on worn cogs, forcing you to replace the cassette and possibly the chainrings at the same time.

Here's how cassette wear works, how to spot it, and how to time your replacements so you're not paying for more than you need to.

![Bicycle cassette showing worn shark-tooth cog teeth and drivetrain close-up on a mountain bike](/images/articles/when-to-replace-cassette.webp)

## The Every-2-to-3-Chains Rule

Your chain wears faster than your cassette. Under normal conditions, one cassette should outlast two to three chains. The logic: replace the chain on schedule (at 0.5% wear on 11/12-speed, 0.75% on ≤10-speed) and the cassette cogs wear gradually and evenly with each chain swap.

Skip a chain replacement — or run one past its threshold — and the worn chain grinds a matching wear profile into the cog teeth. When you install a new, unworn chain on those cogs, the pitch mismatch causes the chain to skip over the teeth under load. At that point, a new chain alone won't fix it. You need a new cassette too.

The cost difference:

| Scenario | Typical Cost |
|---|---|
| Chain replaced on schedule | $30–$60 |
| Chain + cassette (delayed replacement) | $100–$250 |
| Chain + cassette + chainring (very delayed) | $200–$600+ |

Staying on a 2-to-3-chain cassette replacement schedule is the single most effective way to keep drivetrain costs in check.

## Visual Inspection: The Shark-Tooth Test

You don't always need a measurement tool to know a cassette is worn. A visual inspection tells you most of what you need to know.

**Healthy cog teeth**: symmetrical, slightly rounded profile. Both sides of each tooth look the same.

**Worn cog teeth**: asymmetric, leaning toward the direction of chain travel (typically the forward-facing side). The tooth develops a curved, shark-fin shape — narrow and hooked at the tip. This is called the shark-tooth profile and it's a reliable indicator that the cog has worn past its useful life.

How to check: remove the rear wheel and hold the cassette at eye level with good light. Spin it slowly and look at each cog from the side. Focus especially on the cogs you use most — typically the 3–5 smallest cogs, which see the most chain tension during climbing.

A cassette that passes the shark-tooth visual inspection and accepts a new chain without skipping is serviceable. One that fails either test needs replacement.

## Signs of a Worn Cassette While Riding

You don't need to remove the wheel to notice these:

**Chain skipping under load**: the most obvious sign. The chain jumps forward on specific cogs — usually the most-used ones — when you apply hard pedaling effort, especially on climbs. This feels like a sudden lurch or slip, often described as the drivetrain "slipping."

**Poor shifting that doesn't respond to adjustment**: if you've indexed your derailleur correctly, replaced cables, and the shifting is still hesitant or imprecise on specific cogs, worn cassette teeth are often the culprit. Worn cogs have less geometry to guide the chain during shifts.

**Chain skip with a new chain**: if you've just installed a fresh chain and it immediately skips on certain cogs, your cassette is worn. The new chain won't conform to the worn cog profile — you need to replace the cassette alongside the chain.

**Noise under load**: a worn cassette combined with a worn chain often produces a rhythmic crunching or grinding sound under pedaling load, even when the drivetrain appears clean.

## Which Cogs Wear Fastest

Not all cogs on a cassette wear at the same rate. Chain tension is highest when you're in the smallest cogs (hardest gearing), so those see the most wear. Riders who do a lot of climbing in a narrow range of gears — often one or two favorite cogs — will see localized wear on those specific teeth.

On a mountain bike cassette with a wide range (e.g., 10-51 or 11-52 teeth), the smallest three to five cogs typically show wear first. The larger climbing cogs wear much more slowly because chain tension is lower.

This is why a full cassette replacement is usually the right call — even if only two or three cogs are visibly worn, the others will have worn proportionally and the cost difference between replacing two cogs and the full cassette is rarely worth the labor.

## Chainring Replacement: Every 2 to 3 Cassettes

Chainrings outlast cassettes by a large margin — typically by a factor of two to three. That means one chainring per two to three cassette replacements is a reasonable expectation for most riders.

The reason: a chainring has far more teeth than most individual cassette cogs, and the chain moves more slowly across it. The wear per tooth is distributed more widely.

**Visual check for chainrings**: look at the tooth profile from the side. A healthy chainring tooth has a symmetrical, trapezoidal profile (wider at the base, slightly narrower at the tip). Worn teeth develop the same shark-tooth profile as cassette cogs — asymmetric and curved, with the tip leaning in the direction of chain travel.

**Specific signs of worn chainrings**:
- Chain drop on the front: the chain unships off the front ring unexpectedly, especially under sudden load changes
- Chain skip under high power output (distinct from cassette skip — it happens specifically when applying peak torque, like out of the saddle climbing)
- Hesitant front shifting in a 2x drivetrain (though this can also be cable tension or derailleur limit screw issues)

On a 1x drivetrain, chainring wear is more immediately noticeable because there's no secondary ring to fall back on. Budget for a chainring replacement roughly every two cassette swaps.

## Replace Together or Separately?

### Chain + Cassette Together

You should always replace the cassette when you install a new chain if:

1. The cassette shows shark-tooth wear
2. A new chain skips on the cassette immediately
3. You've run the previous chain well past the 0.5%/0.75% threshold

### Chain Only (Cassette Survives)

If you've been replacing chains on schedule — every 2 to 3 chains has been your cassette's history — and the cassette shows no shark-tooth wear, a new chain should run quietly and shift cleanly on the existing cassette. Try the new chain and verify no skipping before buttoning everything up.

### Chain + Cassette + Chainring

This full drivetrain replacement is warranted when the chainring also shows wear, or when you've deferred multiple chain replacements over a long period. It's more cost-effective to do it all at once than to install a new chain and cassette, only to have the worn chainring accelerate wear on the new parts.

## FAQs

### How do I know if I need to replace the cassette or just the chain?

Install a new chain. If it runs quietly without skipping, the cassette is fine. If it skips under load — especially on the most-used cogs — the cassette is worn and needs replacement. The skip test is the most reliable field check.

### Can I replace individual cogs instead of the whole cassette?

On some older cassettes with individually replaceable cogs (mostly Shimano), yes. On modern cassettes where cogs are welded or riveted to a carrier spider, full replacement is required. Even on cassettes with removable cogs, the labor cost usually makes full replacement more economical unless only one cog is worn and the rest are in good shape.

### My bike shifts fine but I can see shark-tooth wear on the cogs. Do I need to replace it?

Yes — soon. Shark-tooth cogs are already past the point where they'll handle a new chain well. The bike may still shift fine because your current chain has worn to match the cog profile. As soon as you replace the chain (which is due at 0.5% wear), you'll likely experience skipping. Replace the cassette and chain together at that point.

### How do I know when the chainring needs replacement vs. just the cassette?

The shark-tooth visual check applies to both. If the cassette shows wear and the chainring shows minimal wear, replace the cassette only. If both show wear — or if you're seeing chain drop or front skip — replace both together. On 1x bikes where chainring replacement is less frequent, it's worth inspecting the ring carefully every cassette swap rather than assuming it's fine.

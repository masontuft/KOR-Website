# Bike Maintenance Schedule: What to Service and When

Your bike will tell you when something is wrong — but by then, the damage is already done. A worn chain has been grinding down your cassette for hundreds of miles. A fork service is three seasons overdue and you've been riding with gritty bushings. A proactive maintenance schedule keeps you on the trail instead of waiting on parts.

Here's the complete guide to what to service, when to service it, and how to stop guessing.

![Cyclist performing maintenance on a clean mountain bike outdoors in natural light](/images/articles/bike-maintenance-schedule.webp)

## Time vs. Mileage vs. Hours: Which Interval System Works?

Most maintenance advice falls into one of three buckets: calendar-based ("every six months"), mileage-based ("every 1,500 miles"), or hour-based ("every 50 hours"). Knowing which system fits which part is the first step to a schedule that actually works.

**Mileage wins for wear parts.** Chain, cassette, brake pads, and tires wear down with use. A rider who covers 5,000 miles a year will hit the chain-replacement window in six months. A rider covering 1,500 miles a year might take two years. Telling both riders to "replace the chain annually" means one is trashing cassettes and the other is spending money they don't need to.

**Hours work for suspension and sealant.** Fork and shock service intervals — and tubeless sealant refresh windows — are based on ride hours, not distance. An hour of technical trail riding puts far more load on a fork than an hour on gravel, regardless of miles covered. Suspension manufacturers set their service windows in hours, and that's the right system.

**Calendar fills the gaps.** Brake fluid, cables, and bearings are less mileage-sensitive. A hydraulic brake bleed every 6–12 months keeps your lever feel consistent whether you've ridden 500 miles or 5,000 in that window.

Use the right system for each part and you'll waste almost nothing.

## Master Maintenance Interval Table

| Component | Interval | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Chain | 1,500–3,000 miles | Replace at 0.5% wear (11/12-speed) or 0.75% (≤10-speed) |
| Cassette | Every 2–3 chains | Skipping under load with a new chain = overdue |
| Chainrings | Every 2–3 cassettes | Inspect for shark-toothed teeth |
| Brake pads (disc) | 500–1,500 miles | Replace when pad material hits 1.5 mm minimum |
| Brake rotors | Per thickness measurement | Typically lasts 2–4 pad sets; measure with calipers |
| Brake bleed | Every 6–12 months | Spongy lever or fade = overdue regardless of calendar |
| Tires | 1,000–3,000 miles | Varies heavily by compound and terrain |
| Tubeless sealant | Every 40–60 ride hours or 2–6 months | Heat and altitude accelerate drying |
| Fork lower-leg service | ~50 ride hours | Full damper service: ~100–200 hours |
| Rear shock air-can service | ~50 ride hours | Full service: ~100–200 hours |
| Dropper post service | ~100–200 ride hours | Sag, slow return, or side play = overdue |
| Bottom bracket | 3,000–10,000 miles | Wide range by bearing type and riding conditions |

These are starting points. Wet and muddy conditions, aggressive riding, and heavier riders push you toward the shorter end of each range. Dry, buffed singletrack often lets you stretch to the longer end.

## Wear Parts: Mileage-Based Intervals

### Chain

Your chain is the canary in the drivetrain. Replace it before it stretches too far and you protect the cassette, chainrings, and derailleur pulleys — parts that cost far more.

At 11- and 12-speed, the replacement threshold is 0.5% wear (measured with a chain checker or a 12-inch ruler). At 10-speed and below, you can run to 0.75%. The cost of waiting: a worn chain accelerates cassette wear exponentially, and a new cassette can cost 10 times what a timely chain replacement would have.

Factors that shorten chain life: wet riding, heavy climbing loads, single-ring setups (chains see more lateral flex), and poor lubrication habits.

### Cassette

Replace your cassette every two to three chains — not when a new chain starts skipping. By the time a new chain skips on the cassette, the cogs are already worn past the economical repair point, and you've likely damaged the chainrings too.

Signs you're overdue: the chain hops under power on the smallest cogs, individual teeth look narrow and curved (shark-tooth profile), and shifting feels hesitant even after a cable adjustment.

### Brake Pads

Disc brake pads have a minimum pad material thickness of 1.5 mm. Once you're below that, braking performance drops sharply — and a pad backing plate grinding on a bare rotor can score the disc so badly it requires replacement too.

Resin pads typically run 500–1,000 miles in normal conditions; metallic or sintered pads can reach 1,500 miles but produce more rotor wear over time. Wet and gritty riding dramatically accelerates wear — check pads every 200–300 miles if you regularly ride in mud or rain, not every 500.

Visual check: look through the caliper from behind the wheel. If the pad material is thin or you're hearing grinding (not just squeal — grinding), stop riding until you inspect.

### Tires

Road tires typically last 1,000–2,500 miles; more durable touring compounds can reach 3,000. MTB tires wear by knob erosion and casing damage rather than a simple mileage counter — a tire with flat-topped, squared-off center knobs has reached end of life for technical terrain, even if the sidewalls look fine.

Wear indicators to watch: squared center knob profile, visible casing threads in the tread area, sidewall cracking, or multiple flats in a short span that can't be explained by road debris.

One important note: do not rotate worn rear tires to the front. The front tire is safety-critical for steering and front-brake traction — a worn rear tire on the front is a handling hazard.

## Hour-Based Service: Suspension and Sealant

### Fork and Rear Shock

Suspension manufacturers set service intervals in ride hours, not miles — and for good reason. A slow technical trail section puts far more stress on a fork than an hour of gravel spinning, regardless of the distance covered.

The general guideline from Fox, RockShox, and most major manufacturers:

- **Lower-leg / air-can service**: approximately every 50 ride hours. This is the accessible service most riders can do at home with basic tooling — oil bath refresh, wiper seal cleaning, air-can inspection and grease.
- **Full damper service**: approximately every 100–200 ride hours. This requires either a qualified mechanic or significant investment in home tooling.

Symptoms of an overdue fork: stiff or grabby small-bump response, oil weeping around the seals, or noticeable stiction when you push down and release the front end. Neglected forks risk bushing and stanchion damage that turns a $150 lower-leg service into a $500+ rebuild.

### Tubeless Sealant

Tubeless sealant dries and clumps over time, losing the ability to self-seal small punctures. The refresh window is **40–60 ride hours** or 2–6 months on the calendar — whichever comes first.

Heat and altitude accelerate drying. Riders in hot climates or high-altitude terrain often need to refresh at the shorter end of that range. The dipstick check: remove the valve core and insert a clean spoke or zip tie. If you come out dry or with solid chunks, it's time.

Top-up vs. full refresh: if the sealant is still liquid but low, topping up is fine. If you're seeing dried clumps or a significant reduction in volume, a full tire removal, cleaning, and fresh charge gives you a reliable baseline.

### Dropper Post

Dropper posts typically need service every 100–200 ride hours. Daily care extends that window: wipe the stanchion clean after every muddy ride and avoid leaving the post in the dropped position for long periods, which can migrate oil past the seals.

Signs of overdue service: the post sags under saddle load, returns slowly to full height, or has developed side play at the collar.

## Calendar-Based Service

### Brake Bleed (Every 6–12 Months)

Hydraulic brake fluid absorbs moisture over time, which lowers its boiling point and creates a spongy, inconsistent lever feel. Mineral oil systems (Shimano, Magura) are less hygroscopic than DOT fluid systems (SRAM, Avid, Hope), but both need regular replacement.

Bleed your brakes when the lever feel goes soft, when you experience brake fade on long descents, or on a 6–12 month interval — whichever comes first. This is a natural annual shop service item if you're not set up to bleed at home.

### Cables and Housing

Brake and derailleur cables fray from the inside out; you often can't see damage until the cable snaps mid-ride. Housing breaks down and creates friction that no barrel adjuster can fix. Annual cable replacement is a reasonable baseline for high-mileage riders; every two years works for lighter use.

Signs of needed replacement: shifting that doesn't respond to barrel adjuster tweaks, a brake lever that doesn't fully return, or visible fraying at anchor bolts.

### Bottom Bracket

BB lifespan varies widely — 3,000–10,000 miles depending on bearing quality, riding conditions, and BB standard. Press-fit BBs in frames with tight tolerances can last well; loose fits or frames with flex cause rapid wear.

Check periodically for play (grab the crankarm and push/pull it laterally) and roughness (spin the cranks with the chain off and feel for grittiness or notchiness). Creaking is often the first symptom — though creaking can also originate from pedals, seatpost, saddle rails, or chainring bolts before you narrow it down to the BB.

## Weekly, Monthly, and Seasonal Checklists

### Before Every Ride (2 minutes)

- Tire pressure — use a floor pump with a gauge, not a squeeze
- Brake lever pull — no spongy feel, pads reach the rotor promptly
- Quick spin of both wheels — nothing rubbing, axles seated fully
- Dropper actuates cleanly, saddle height correct

### Monthly (15–20 minutes)

- Clean and re-lube the chain
- Check chain wear with a chain checker tool
- Inspect tires for embedded debris and sidewall cracks
- Check brake pad thickness through the caliper from behind
- Wipe down frame, pivots, and suspension stanchions

### Every Three to Six Months

- Inspect cables and housing for fraying and sluggish movement
- Check derailleur hanger — a bent hanger is the most common cause of poor shifting
- Inspect brake rotors for contamination and measure thickness
- Check cassette visually for shark-tooth cog profile
- Inspect fork seals and add fresh suspension fluid if seeping

### Annually / End of Season

- Full drivetrain clean: degrease chainrings, derailleur pulleys, and cassette
- Brake bleed (hydraulic systems)
- Review suspension hours and service if approaching interval
- Bottom bracket check for play and roughness
- Full tubeless sealant refresh if not done recently

## FAQs

### How often should I service my bike?

At minimum: clean and lube the chain monthly (or every 100–200 miles in dry conditions, more in wet riding), check brake pad thickness every 300–500 miles, and complete a seasonal full-service covering drivetrain, brakes, cables, and fork seals. High-mileage riders (5,000+ miles per year) benefit most from tracking components by mileage rather than calendar.

### Is bike maintenance worth it?

Yes — and the math is clear. A chain costs $30–$60. A cassette costs $60–$200. One timely chain replacement can prevent two or three premature cassette replacements. Beyond the cost angle, a well-maintained bike shifts cleanly, brakes predictably, and is simply more enjoyable to ride. The components that feel "worn in" are often just worn out.

### Can I skip maintenance if the bike feels fine?

Wear doesn't always feel like wear until you've crossed a threshold. A chain can be stretched well past 0.5% and shifting will still work — until you install a new chain and it immediately skips on the worn cassette. Rotors often feel fine until they drop below minimum thickness and braking suddenly falls off. Condition-based checks are useful, but mileage-based intervals catch the problems before they become expensive repairs.

### What happens if I skip suspension service?

Seals dry out and contaminants get in. Those contaminants — typically fine grit and degraded oil — act as a lapping compound on your stanchion tubes. Stanchion scoring is expensive. A lower-leg service at 50 hours runs $80–$150 at most shops; a stanchion replacement on a mid-range fork can run $300–$600. Service on schedule and you protect a large investment.

# When to Replace Bike Brake Rotors (Thickness Guide)

Replace your bike's disc brake rotors when the rotor face wears below the manufacturer's minimum thickness — typically **1.5 mm**, stamped directly on the rotor's outer edge. Beyond that threshold, the steel is too thin to dissipate braking heat safely and braking performance drops sharply. A contaminated, warped, or badly scored rotor needs to come out before it reaches that measurement.

Most rotors outlast two to four sets of brake pads. That means if you're disciplined about replacing pads on schedule, a single rotor can last 3,000–6,000 miles or more. The goal here is knowing the signs that cut that life short.

## The Minimum Thickness Number: Where to Find It

Every disc brake rotor has a minimum thickness requirement set by the manufacturer. You'll find it:

- **Stamped or engraved on the rotor** — look at the outer edge of the disc. Common values: 1.5 mm (most MTB rotors), 1.6 mm (some road rotors), 1.8 mm (some larger rotors).
- **In the manufacturer's spec sheet** — search the brand name and rotor model number if you can't read the stamping.

When in doubt, use **1.5 mm** as the working minimum if you can't find the stamped number. That's the floor for the majority of MTB and gravel rotors from Shimano, SRAM, and most OEM brands.

A rotor at or below minimum thickness is a safety issue, not just a performance one. Too-thin steel flexes under braking force, can warp under heat, and in extreme cases can crack. Replace it before that point.

## How to Measure Rotor Thickness with Calipers

You'll need a set of digital or vernier calipers — this is a quick job once you have them.

1. Remove the wheel from the bike. You can measure with the wheel on, but off is easier to get a clean reading.
2. Clean the rotor's braking surface with isopropyl alcohol and a clean rag. Grit or contamination throws off the measurement.
3. Open the calipers and place the measuring faces on opposite sides of the rotor's braking track — the shiny area where pads contact the disc.
4. Take readings in three or four spots around the rotor. Wear is often uneven; the thinnest reading is the one that matters.
5. Compare your lowest reading to the stamped minimum. If you're at or below minimum, the rotor is done.

New rotors typically measure 1.8–2.3 mm depending on size and brand. Replacing around 1.6–1.7 mm gives you some buffer before hitting the hard limit. Waiting until the last fraction of a millimeter isn't necessary or wise.

## Three Reasons a Rotor Needs Replacement

Thickness isn't the only reason to pull a rotor. Three separate problems push a rotor past its useful life.

### 1. Wear (Thickness)

Normal friction wears rotor steel from both faces simultaneously. The braking track becomes slightly concave — a groove worn into the surface where pads contact it. That profile is normal and not itself a problem until the remaining thickness drops below minimum. Check with calipers every 1–2 years or whenever you're replacing pads.

### 2. Warping

A warped rotor produces a rhythmic rubbing or pulsing under braking — you feel it through the lever and sometimes through the bike. Warping is caused by heat: sustained braking on long descents, cooling a hot rotor in water (stream crossings, heavy rain), or setting a hot bike on a surface that contacts the rotor.

Minor warps can sometimes be trued (more on that below). Severe warps — where the rotor deflects more than about 0.5 mm at the caliper — typically mean replacement. And a rotor that warps once under normal conditions is often weaker steel that'll warp again.

### 3. Contamination

Oil, chain lube, or brake fluid on a rotor causes brake fade, inconsistent feel, and a squealing or grabby bite that doesn't respond to cleaning. Contamination on the rotor soaks into the pad material; once a pad is contaminated, it usually can't be recovered — replace both together.

If you contaminated a rotor that's otherwise at good thickness, start with a thorough clean using isopropyl alcohol and a new microfiber cloth. Spray cleaners aren't recommended — the propellant can leave a film. If the rotor still performs poorly after cleaning and a new pad set, replace it.

## Rotor Lifespan: ≈ 2–4 Pad Sets

As a practical benchmark, expect **one rotor to last roughly two to four sets of brake pads**. That works out to:

- **Resin pads (500–1,000 mi per set)**: One rotor lasts approximately 1,500–4,000 miles
- **Metallic pads (1,000–1,500 mi per set)**: One rotor lasts approximately 2,000–6,000 miles

Metallic pads are harder on rotors than resin; they accelerate rotor wear slightly in exchange for longer pad life. The tradeoff is usually worth it for aggressive or wet-season riding, but worth knowing.

Heavy descending, grit, and poor riding conditions compress all these numbers. A rotor that's seen several seasons of alpine riding will measure thinner than one that's logged the same mileage on smooth gravel paths.

For details on how brake pad replacement timing interacts with rotor life, see [When to Replace Bike Brake Pads](/articles/when-to-replace-brake-pads).

## Truing vs. Replacing

A trued rotor is bent back into alignment to eliminate a warp. Replacing means pulling the disc entirely. Here's how to decide:

**Try truing first if:**
- The rotor is at good thickness and in otherwise good condition
- The warp is minor — slight rub at one spot in the caliper, not a full-cycle pulsation
- The rotor has not been contaminated

**Replace immediately if:**
- Thickness is at or near minimum
- The warp is severe — visible deflection, strong pulsation through the lever
- The disc is cracked, badly scored, or has deep grooves from metal-on-metal contact
- Contamination has compromised the braking surface and cleaning didn't help
- The rotor has warped more than once under normal riding conditions

Truing a rotor is a valid repair for minor alignment issues, but it doesn't restore thickness or fix contamination. If you're already at the upper end of pad replacement frequency, it's often smarter to swap the rotor now and get a clean start.

## Checking Both Pads and Rotor Together

Ideally you're checking rotor thickness every time you replace pads — it takes 30 seconds with calipers. Build this habit:

- Replace pads when they reach 1.5 mm — don't wait for grinding
- When you pull the pads, measure the rotor
- If the rotor is within 0.2–0.3 mm of its minimum, replace it now rather than making another trip when it finishes wearing

## FAQs

### What is the minimum thickness for a bike brake rotor?

Most MTB and gravel disc brake rotors have a minimum thickness of **1.5 mm**, stamped on the outer edge of the disc. Road rotors and some larger-diameter discs may specify 1.6–1.8 mm. Always check the stamped value on your rotor; if you can't read it, use 1.5 mm as the conservative floor.

### How do I know if my rotors are warped?

A warped rotor creates a rhythmic rubbing, pulsing, or squealing sound that follows each wheel rotation — you'll feel it in the brake lever and sometimes through the handlebars. The sensation repeats once per wheel revolution. Minor warps can sometimes be trued; severe warps or frequently recurring warps usually mean replacement.

### How long do bike brake rotors last?

Expect a rotor to last roughly two to four sets of brake pads — around 2,000–6,000 miles depending on pad compound, riding conditions, and how hard the brakes run. Heavy descending, wet and gritty conditions, and metallic pads all accelerate rotor wear.

### Can I replace just the rotors, or do I need new pads too?

If you're replacing a worn-out rotor, swap the pads at the same time — they've broken in together and new pads bed in better on a fresh disc surface. If the rotor and pads are both contaminated, you must replace both: contaminated pad material doesn't recover even on a clean rotor.

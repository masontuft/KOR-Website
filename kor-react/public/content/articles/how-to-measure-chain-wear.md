# How to Measure Chain Wear: Checker Tool & Ruler Methods

Measuring chain wear takes about five seconds with the right tool. At 0.5% wear on an 11- or 12-speed chain (0.75% on 10-speed and below), it's time to replace. Here's how to get that reading accurately — and what to do with it.

## What You're Actually Measuring

"Chain stretch" is the common term, but what actually happens is that the pins and inner link plates wear down over thousands of miles of use. As material is lost, the effective pitch of each link increases — the chain gets slightly longer across a given number of links. That elongation is what chain checkers and ruler measurements detect.

A new chain has a link pitch of exactly 0.5 inches (half an inch per link). At 0.5% wear, the chain has effectively lengthened by 0.5% over its length. That sounds small, but it's enough to throw the chain out of sync with your cassette cogs and chainrings, which are designed to a precise, unworn pitch.

The key insight: the chain may still feel functional and shift well at 0.5% wear. The damage is happening invisibly to the cassette. Catching wear at this threshold protects parts that cost 3–10x what the chain does.

## Method 1: Chain Checker Tool (Recommended)

A dedicated chain checker is the fastest and most reliable method. They cost $10–$25 and last for years of regular use.

### What You Need

- A chain wear indicator tool (CC-3.2, Park Tool CC-4, Shimano TL-CN42, or any similar two-probe checker)

### Step-by-Step

**Step 1: Clean the chain.** Wipe off surface grime with a rag or brush. Heavy buildup on the links can give a false reading by adding material between the checker probes and the link pins.

**Step 2: Locate the check position.** Place the chain in the largest chainring and let it hang on the bottom or side — anywhere the chain is under no tension and hangs straight.

**Step 3: Hook the first probe.** Insert the fixed end of the checker into a roller gap (between two links). This anchors the tool against a pin.

**Step 4: Apply the wear probe.** Lower the second probe (the 0.5 side for 11/12-speed, or the 0.75 side for 10-speed and below) toward the chain.

**Step 5: Read the result.**
- **Probe drops into a roller gap under its own weight**: the chain has reached that wear level. Replace the chain.
- **Probe rests on top of the link**: the chain is within spec. Re-check in 200–300 miles.

Some checkers use a sliding scale with 0.5 and 0.75 marks on one tool. The principle is the same — if the indicator can seat at 0.5, replace the chain.

**Step 6: Check multiple spots.** Chains don't always wear uniformly. Check three to five different sections, especially if you've seen any skipping or suspect a kinked link. If any section reads worn, the whole chain needs replacement.

### Reading the Result

| Reading | Action |
|---|---|
| 0.5 probe drops in (11/12-speed) | Replace chain now |
| 0.75 probe drops in (≤10-speed) | Replace chain now |
| Neither probe drops | Check again in 200–300 miles (wet riding) or 300–500 miles (dry riding) |

## Method 2: 12-Inch Ruler Method

If you don't have a chain checker, a quality steel ruler works. This method is slightly less precise but perfectly adequate for regular checks.

### What You Need

- A steel ruler with clear 1/16-inch markings (a flexible tape measure is less accurate — use a rigid ruler if possible)

### Step-by-Step

**Step 1: Clean the chain.** As with the checker method, surface grit can slightly affect measurement.

**Step 2: Place the ruler.** Align the zero mark of the ruler with the center of a link pin. The pin center is the small round circle visible in the middle of each outer plate.

**Step 3: Measure to the 12-inch mark.** Look at where the 12-inch mark falls relative to the nearest link pin center. On a new chain, the pin center sits almost exactly at the 12-inch mark.

**Step 4: Read stretch.**
- **Pin center at or before 12 inches**: chain is within spec
- **Pin center between 12 and 12-1/16 inches**: approaching 0.5% wear — check again soon
- **Pin center at or beyond 12-1/16 inches**: 0.5% or greater wear — replace chain (11/12-speed)
- **Pin center at or beyond 12-1/8 inches**: 0.75% or greater wear — replace chain (≤10-speed)

**Step 5: Check multiple sections.** Repeat the measurement at three to five points around the chain to catch any locally worn sections.

### Ruler Method: Key Measurements

| Measurement at 12-inch start | Wear level | Action (11/12-speed) | Action (≤10-speed) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pin at 12 inches exactly | ~0% | Good | Good |
| Pin at 12-1/16 inches | ~0.5% | Replace | Monitor |
| Pin at 12-1/8 inches | ~0.75% | Replace | Replace |
| Pin at 12-1/4 inches | ~1.0% | Replace immediately | Replace immediately |

## Checking for Roller Wear vs. Pin Wear

Chain stretch measures elongation, but chains also wear in a second way: the rollers that contact the cassette cogs can wear down, becoming oval or developing flat spots. This wear doesn't always show on an elongation check.

To check roller wear: remove the chain and hold a section up to light. Roll each roller with your fingers — worn rollers feel rough or notchy and may show visible flat spots. If you're seeing this kind of wear alongside elongation at or past threshold, replace the chain and inspect your cassette closely for matching cog wear.

## How Often to Check

Build chain-wear checking into your regular bike wash routine:

- **Wet or muddy conditions**: check every 200–300 miles
- **Dry, normal conditions**: check every 300–500 miles
- **After any unusual drivetrain noise**: check immediately

Catching wear at the threshold — not past it — is the whole point. A chain in spec one week and over 0.5% the next is normal; the wear curve accelerates near the end of chain life.

## What to Do When You Replace the Chain

When a chain hits threshold:

1. Remove the old chain (chain breaker tool or master link)
2. Clean the cassette and chainrings thoroughly — grit on the cogs accelerates new chain wear
3. Inspect the cassette: if you're replacing the chain on schedule (every 2–3 chains), the cassette should be fine. If this is the first chain replacement on an older cassette, check for shark-toothed cog profiles
4. Install the new chain at the correct length (same as the old chain, unless the drivetrain has changed)
5. Log the mileage reset — this is where tracking tools become valuable

## FAQs

### Which chain checker should I buy?

The Park Tool CC-3.2 and Shimano TL-CN42 are the two most commonly recommended options. Both use a two-probe design and last for years. The Park Tool CC-4 adds a 0.3% indicator useful for catching wear very early. Any checker from a reputable tool brand that has clearly marked 0.5 and 0.75 probes will do the job.

### Can I measure chain wear with a caliper?

Yes — but it's more involved than a ruler check and not significantly more accurate for field use. If you have digital calipers, measure the distance between 24 links (rivet center to rivet center): a new chain spans 304.8 mm. At 0.5% wear, that measurement reads approximately 306.3 mm. Useful for a workshop check, but a dedicated chain checker is easier for regular use.

### My new chain skips on the cassette. Is the chain defective?

Probably not — this is almost always a sign that the cassette is worn and has conformed to the shape of the previous (stretched) chain. A worn cassette has shallow, shaped cog teeth that won't mesh properly with a new, unworn chain. Inspect the cassette for shark-tooth cog profiles. The fix is a new cassette alongside the new chain.

### Does chain wear measurement differ for single-speed bikes?

Single-speed chains wear similarly but there's less consequence for the cassette (typically just a single sprocket). The same 0.5%/0.75% measurement applies; on a single-speed you're mostly protecting the rear sprocket and chainring from accelerated wear. Check at the same mileage intervals.

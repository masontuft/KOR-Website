# When to Replace Bike Brake Pads: Wear Signs & Intervals

Replace disc brake pads when pad material drops to 1.5 mm or less — most resin pads hit that point in 500–1,000 miles, sintered or metallic pads in 1,000–1,500 miles. Wait past that threshold and the metal backing plate contacts the rotor, scoring the disc and turning a $20 pad swap into a $60+ rotor replacement.

Here's how to know when you're there, what warning signs to watch for, and how to get the most out of a new set.

![Mountain bike disc brake rotor and caliper close-up detail on a trail](/images/articles/when-to-replace-brake-pads.webp)

## Disc Brake Pad Lifespan: Resin vs. Metallic

Brake pads come in two main compounds, and they wear at very different rates.

**Resin (organic) pads** use a softer compound that's quiet, easy on rotors, and provides good initial bite. Typical lifespan is **500–1,000 miles**. Resin pads tolerate light rain well but wear faster in sustained wet and gritty conditions — a single muddy trail ride can put a noticeable dent in pad life.

**Metallic (sintered) pads** are harder, more heat-resistant, and last **1,000–1,500 miles**. They run louder and cause more rotor wear over time, but they hold up better on long descents and in technical riding where brakes see sustained heat. Preferred for enduro, DH, and any riding where consistent braking through heat is a priority.

**Semi-metallic (hybrid) pads** split the difference in both lifespan and performance — a good everyday option for mixed-terrain riding.

Conditions that push you toward the short end of any range:

- Wet, muddy, or sandy trails (grit acts as an abrasive between pad and rotor)
- Long, sustained descents with heavy braking
- Higher rider and bike total weight
- Neglected cleaning — grit packed around the caliper keeps grinding between rides

## The 1.5 mm Minimum

The universal disc brake pad replacement threshold is **1.5 mm of pad material remaining** — measured from the pad face to the metal backing plate. Most manufacturers stamp this as the minimum on pad spec sheets, and it's a hard limit: below 1.5 mm, braking performance degrades and metal-on-metal contact becomes likely.

How to check:

1. Look through the caliper from the non-rotor side (behind the wheel). Both pads sandwich the rotor — you can see pad thickness from this angle.
2. If the pad material looks paper-thin — roughly the thickness of a coin or less — pull the wheel and measure.
3. Retract the pistons slightly, pull the pads, and measure with a ruler or calipers. Under 1.5 mm? Replace them.

Many pads have a **wear indicator groove** down the center of the pad face. When that groove disappears, you're at or near minimum. This is your everyday visual check — no tools needed.

## Noise, Lever Feel, and Visual Warning Signs

You don't need to measure every ride. These symptoms tell you to stop and inspect immediately:

**Grinding or metallic scraping**: High-pitched squeal is normal with cold or new pads. A metallic scraping or grinding sound under braking is not — it means pad material is gone and backing plate is contacting rotor. Stop riding. Every pedal stroke with that sound is scoring your disc.

**Reduced stopping power**: If you're pulling the lever further to get the same deceleration, or braking distance has noticeably increased, pads are either worn or contaminated. Contamination (oil, chain lube, spray-on cleaner that misted onto the rotor) mimics wear — inspect before assuming you need new pads.

**Inconsistent bite**: A lever that feels inconsistent or grabby is more often a brake fluid issue than worn pads — see [How Often to Bleed Disc Brakes](/articles/how-often-bleed-disc-brakes). If the lever also feels like it's traveling further than usual, worn pads are likely involved.

**Glazed pads**: Overheated pads can harden and lose friction without wearing thin. Glazed pads have a shiny, glassy surface instead of a matte compound texture. Light sanding or a firm bedding cycle sometimes recovers them; badly glazed pads are better replaced.

## Rim Brake Pads: Different Indicators

Rim brakes use rubber pads that contact the wheel's braking surface. The wear signs are similar in principle but different in detail:

- Most rim pads have **molded wear grooves** in the rubber. When the grooves disappear, the pad is at end of life and should be replaced before it contacts the metal shoe.
- The pad should sit parallel to the rim braking track. A pad that contacts the tire or angles badly away from the rim needs realignment — not just replacement.
- Inspect the rubber surface for embedded grit or metal particles. These act as abrasives and accelerate wear on both pad and rim. Remove debris with a pick or replace the pads.
- **Carbon rims require carbon-specific pads**. Standard rubber pads can delaminate a carbon braking surface. Never substitute.

Rim pads typically last 1,000–3,000 miles in dry conditions. Rain and grit shorten that range significantly.

## Wet and Gritty Conditions: Check More Often

Wet riding isn't just harder on pads — the wear rate is disproportionately higher. Grit and sand suspended in water get trapped between pad and rotor and act as lapping compound, grinding both surfaces faster than any dry trail would.

In wet conditions:

- **Check pads every 200–300 miles** rather than the usual 500-mile interval
- Expect resin pads to reach end-of-life at the low end (500 miles or less in a bad season)
- Consider switching to metallic pads for extended wet-season riding
- Rinse the caliper area after muddy rides — caked grit keeps grinding long after you're home

## Bedding In New Brake Pads

Fresh pads on a rotor don't perform at full power until a thin, even layer of pad material has transferred onto the rotor surface. This bedding process takes about 10 minutes and makes a real difference in feel and braking distance.

Standard bedding procedure:

1. From about 15 mph, apply firm (not full-emergency) braking pressure until you're nearly stopped — don't lock the wheel or skid.
2. Release and let the brakes cool for 30–60 seconds between passes.
3. Repeat 8–10 times. By pass five or six, the brake feel should be noticeably more solid.

For new pads on new rotors, or brake systems with aggressive compound, increase to 8–10 passes from 20+ mph at around 70% effort.

Avoid dragging brakes at low speed during bedding — this deposits compound unevenly and causes vibration. After the session, pads may smoke lightly; that's normal. If you get extreme fade or a burning rubber smell immediately, back off the effort and let everything cool completely before continuing.

## FAQs

### How do I know if my brake pads are worn out?

Look through the caliper from the non-rotor side — you can see pad material sandwiching the rotor. If the pads look paper-thin, or the wear indicator groove on the pad face has disappeared, it's time to replace. Grinding or metallic scraping under braking means you're already at or past the limit.

### How often should I replace bike brake pads?

Resin pads typically need replacement every 500–1,000 miles; metallic or sintered pads every 1,000–1,500 miles. Wet and gritty riding shortens both ranges. Check pad thickness visually every 300–500 miles in normal conditions, every 200–300 miles during wet riding seasons.

### Can I replace just the pads, or do I need new rotors too?

If you caught the pads before they reached metal-on-metal contact, the rotor is likely fine. If you've been riding with a grinding noise, inspect the rotor for scoring or deep grooves, and measure its thickness. A rotor worn below manufacturer minimum or badly scored needs to come out. See [When to Replace Bike Brake Rotors](/articles/when-to-replace-brake-rotors).

### Do I need to bed in brake pads every time I replace them?

Yes — every new pad set needs a bedding cycle, even if the rotors are reused. Skipping bedding-in leads to inconsistent bite, vibration, and noise. The process takes 10 minutes and produces dramatically better braking feel.
